# Template: Blog Post / Case Study

> **Audience:** General / Public (technical to non-technical; calibrate per brief)
> **Goal:** Educate / Persuade
> **Length:** 800–2000 words
>
> **Author instructions:** A blog post earns attention; it does not demand it. The Hook must make a specific reader want to continue. Every section must pull the reader forward. Show thinking, not just results — readers engage with the reasoning behind decisions, not just the conclusions. End with something actionable or resonant.
>
> Calibrate technical density using the audience tier from the doc-brief: a practitioner-focused post (Tier 1) can use precise technical language; a general-interest post (Tier 3) should translate and use analogy.
>
> **Remove all template instructions (lines starting with `>`) from the final document.**

---

# [Post Title]

## [Hook]

> Do not use "Hook" as a section heading — lead directly into the content. Open with a question that creates tension, a specific problem the reader recognises, a surprising fact, or a concrete scenario. The first paragraph decides whether the reader continues. Do not start with "In this post I will..." or "Today I want to talk about..."
>
> 1–3 paragraphs. Make the reader feel the problem before offering the answer.

## Background

> Enough context for a reader unfamiliar with the work to follow the story. What did the landscape look like before this? What is the domain? What would a reader need to know to understand the choices made?
>
> Calibrate to audience tier: a technical reader needs domain context, not definitions. A general reader may need both. Do not over-explain to a technical reader; do not under-explain to a general one.

## The Approach

> The longest section. What was done and why. Show the thinking, not just the steps — the reader wants to understand the reasoning, not follow a tutorial. Walk through key decisions. Acknowledge dead ends or alternatives considered. Make it feel like a real project, not a cleaned-up success story.
>
> This is where technical content lives. Use code, diagrams, or examples when they clarify rather than just demonstrate.

## The Results

> What happened. Be concrete and specific. Numbers, test output, comparisons. Avoid vague claims. If results were mixed or limited, say so — honesty builds credibility with a technical audience.

## What We Learned

> The transferable insight. What does this project reveal about the problem space, the approach, or the tools? What would someone building something similar need to know? This section is what turns a project writeup into a piece people share.
>
> 1–2 paragraphs. Do not summarise the sections above.

## Where to Go Next

> Invite engagement. Point to the repository, a follow-on question worth exploring, a related piece of work, or the next post in a series. Make it easy for the reader to take a next step.
>
> 1–3 sentences. Do not pad.
